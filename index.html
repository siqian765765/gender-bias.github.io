Fairness in scholarly publishing

Peer Review and Gender Bias

Evidence from submission and peer-review records across 145 scholarly journals, focusing on gender representation among authors and reviewers, review scores, and final editorial decisions.

Data & Method

Data and Analytical Variables

The analysis links manuscript submissions, author composition, reviewer assignment, review recommendations, and final editorial decisions.

Submission and peer-review records from 145 journals across biomedicine and health, life sciences, physical sciences, and social sciences and humanities.

Female author share, female reviewer share, review score, reviewer agreement, review type, journal impact factor, desk rejection, and final acceptance.

Records are grouped by discipline to compare author and reviewer gender composition, while preserving the flow from submission to final decision.

Findings and Limitations

The evidence does not show a stable, systematic penalty against women authors after manuscripts enter peer review. This does not mean scholarly publishing is gender-equal overall.

01

Main Finding

Women remain underrepresented in scholarly publishing: female author shares are lower, female reviewer participation is limited, and women are also less represented in editorial roles.

However, after manuscripts enter peer review, the study does not find a persistent, systematic penalty against women authors.

Context Women are underrepresented among authors, reviewers, and editors.
Core Result No systematic penalty against women authors is detected within peer review.
02

Scope of Interpretation

The result applies to manuscripts that have already entered peer review; it does not cover all earlier selection processes.

Gender inequality may still operate before submission through research resources, networks, promotion, and long-term retention.

03

Reviewer Assignment

According to the original study, reviewer assignment shows some gender homophily: manuscripts with higher shares of women authors are more likely to be assigned women reviewers.

This indicates that reviewer selection is not fully gender-neutral, although the study does not find that this pattern translates into a systematic penalty against women authors.

04

Disciplinary Differences

Review recommendations

Model results suggest that the relationship between women-author share and review recommendations varies by discipline, with positive patterns in biomedicine and social sciences but weaker or negative patterns in other fields.

Editorial decisions

Editorial decisions do not follow the same pattern as reviewer recommendations: some fields show higher predicted acceptance for manuscripts with more women authors, while others show no stable effect.

The key point is that reviewer recommendations and editorial decisions are not equivalent. Gender-related patterns can differ across disciplines and across stages of the review process.
05

Related Evidence

Fairness in peer review is not only reflected in final acceptance. It can also appear in reviewer assignment, author visibility, institutional prestige, and review anonymity.

Helmer et al. (2017) Women are underrepresented among editors and reviewers, and reviewer selection shows gender homophily.
Tomkins et al. (2017) Single-blind review can advantage well-known authors and authors from high-prestige institutions.
Witteman et al. (2019) Women applicants are disadvantaged when evaluation focuses on applicant track record rather than proposal quality.
Kern-Goldberger et al. (2022) Double-blind review may reduce gender bias, although empirical findings are not fully consistent.
06

Limitations and Implications

Model limits

The model explains only part of the variation in review recommendations. Manuscript quality, novelty, author reputation, and institutional resources are difficult to measure fully.

Identity variables

The analysis focuses mainly on gender and does not fully incorporate race, region, socioeconomic background, or other intersecting identities.

The value of the study is not to claim that gender inequality is absent, but to locate where inequality is more likely to emerge: often before or outside the peer-review stage itself.
